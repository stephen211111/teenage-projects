## 120W Blue Diode Laser Array

Design and build of a high-power blue diode laser array producing approximately 120W of combined optical output.

Blue diode lasers (445–450nm wavelength) have strong absorption in metals and are well-suited for materials processing applications. This project involved sourcing, driving, and thermally managing a multi-emitter array to reach meaningful continuous power output.

**What was built:**
- Multi-emitter array combining individual blue laser diodes
- Driver electronics for current-controlled operation
- Thermal management system (heatsinking, active cooling)
- Optical alignment and beam combining

**Key challenges:** Driving high-power diode arrays requires precise current control — diodes are sensitive to overcurrent and thermal runaway. Beam combining from multiple emitters without excessive divergence loss was the primary optical challenge.

**Contents:** Build documentation and photographs, circuit schematics for driver electronics, thermal calculations and cooling design notes, test results and measured output power.

*Built as a teenager as part of broader interest in laser systems and materials processing. Preceded and informed later work on selective laser melting and metal additive manufacturing.*

## SLS Metal 3D Printer

Attempted design and partial construction of a selective laser sintering (SLS) metal 3D printer. The build was not completed, but the engineering work and documentation are preserved here.

Selective laser sintering fuses metal powder layer by layer using a focused laser beam. Building a machine capable of this requires solving problems across precision motion, high-power laser handling, powder bed management, inert atmosphere control, and thermal management — simultaneously. This project was an attempt to do that with limited resources and no institutional support.

**What was designed and partially built:**
- Motion system design: gantry and powder bed mechanics
- Laser system integration (building on prior blue diode laser array work)
- Powder handling and recoating mechanism design
- Control system architecture
- Inert atmosphere enclosure concept

**Why it wasn't completed:** The primary blockers were achieving sufficient laser power density at the focal point for metal sintering, and powder bed temperature control. These are non-trivial engineering problems even in industrial systems. The project was paused rather than abandoned — the learning fed directly into later work on SLM scan strategies and the additive manufacturing patent.

**Contents:** Design drawings and CAD files (partial), engineering notes and calculations, build photographs (partial assembly), failure analysis and identified blockers.

*Ambitious independent project undertaken as a teenager. Incomplete, but the attempt and the documented engineering reasoning are the point. The SLM knowledge developed here directly informed subsequent research into scan strategies for selective laser melting and the filed patent on metal additive manufacturing devices.*

## PVD Chamber

Design and construction of two iterations of a physical vapor deposition (PVD) chamber for thin film deposition.

Physical vapor deposition produces thin films by evaporating or sputtering a source material in vacuum and allowing it to condense onto a substrate. Applications include optical coatings, conductive films, and surface modification. Building a functional PVD chamber from scratch requires solving problems across vacuum engineering, high-voltage electronics, and materials handling simultaneously.

**Iterations:**

*i1 — First build:* Initial chamber design. Focus on achieving and sustaining rough vacuum sufficient for thermal evaporation. Identified limitations in pumping speed and chamber geometry that informed the redesign.

*i2 — Second build:* Revised chamber addressing i1 shortcomings. Improved vacuum sealing, modified electrode geometry, better feedthrough design.

**What was built:**
- Vacuum chamber fabrication and sealing
- Roughing pump integration and plumbing
- High-voltage feedthroughs for evaporation source
- Substrate mounting and positioning
- Instrumentation: vacuum gauge, temperature monitoring

**Contents:** Design drawings and CAD files for both iterations, build photographs (i1 and i2), vacuum system notes and measured pump-down curves, lessons learned between iterations.

*Independent project undertaken as a teenager. The iterative approach (i1 → i2) reflects a systematic attempt to diagnose and address failure modes rather than abandon the project.*

## Laser-Induced Graphene Supercapacitors

Self-directed research project exploring the fabrication of supercapacitors using laser-induced graphene (LIG) derived from Kapton (polyimide) tape.

Laser irradiation of polyimide film converts the surface layer into porous graphene — a process that requires no chemical reduction steps and produces electrodes directly suitable for electrochemical energy storage. This project investigated whether consumer-accessible laser equipment could produce functional LIG electrodes and whether these could be assembled into measurable supercapacitors.

**What was done:**
- CO₂ laser irradiation of Kapton tape at varied power and scan speed parameters
- Electrode characterisation: visual inspection, basic conductivity measurement
- Supercapacitor assembly using gel electrolyte
- Electrochemical testing: cyclic voltammetry, charge-discharge curves
- Written scientific report documenting methodology and results

**Contents:** Scientific report (PDF), experimental photographs, raw measurement data, parameter sweep notes (laser power, scan speed, pass count).

*This was an independent project undertaken as a teenager, prior to any formal university training. Equipment access was self-arranged. The work was documented to scientific report standard.*
